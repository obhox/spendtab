Late payments are one of the biggest reasons small businesses in Nigeria fail — not because they're not making sales, but because the money isn't coming in fast enough to cover costs. And in many cases, the invoice itself is part of the problem.

A poorly structured invoice signals to a client that they can delay. A professional one signals that you run a serious business — and that you'll follow up.

Here's how to create invoices that get you paid faster.

## What Makes an Invoice "Professional"?

A professional invoice isn't just a well-designed document. It's one that contains the right information, is delivered promptly, and leaves the client with zero excuses to delay payment.

At minimum, every invoice you send should include:

- **Your business name and contact details** — company name, address, email, phone number
- **Your client's name and address** — billing contact, not just the company name
- **A unique invoice number** — for your records and theirs
- **Invoice date and due date** — make the deadline explicit
- **Itemised list of services or goods** — with quantity, unit price, and line totals
- **Subtotal, applicable taxes (VAT), and grand total**
- **Payment instructions** — bank account number, bank name, and account name
- **Your payment terms** — e.g. "Payment due within 7 days"

Missing any of these gives a slow-paying client a reason to come back with questions — which delays payment further.

## VAT: When Do You Include It?

If your business is registered for VAT with the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS), you are legally required to charge 7.5% VAT on taxable goods and services, and to show it separately on your invoice.

If you're not yet VAT-registered (registration is mandatory above ₦25 million annual turnover), do not charge VAT — adding it when you're not registered is illegal.

When including VAT, structure your totals like this:

| | |
|---|---|
| Subtotal | ₦200,000 |
| VAT (7.5%) | ₦15,000 |
| **Total Due** | **₦215,000** |

Your VAT registration number (TIN) should also appear on the invoice.

## Setting Payment Terms That Work

"Payment upon receipt" is vague. "Net 30" is too generous for most small businesses. Here's what actually works for Nigerian SMEs:

- **Net 7** for services under ₦500,000 — short enough to maintain cash flow
- **50% upfront, 50% on delivery** for larger projects — protects you from non-payment
- **Net 14 or Net 30** for established corporate clients — match their payment cycles to avoid friction

State your terms clearly on the invoice. Clients ignore what isn't written down.

## The Fastest Way to Lose an Invoice

Email it to the wrong person.

Before you send, confirm:
1. Who specifically approves invoices at that company
2. Whether they need a PO (purchase order) number attached
3. Whether to CC anyone in accounts payable

Large organisations especially will park an invoice for weeks if it hits the wrong inbox. One phone call before you send can save you 30 days of waiting.

## Following Up Without Feeling Awkward

Most overdue payments in Nigeria aren't malicious — they're just low on someone's priority list. A structured follow-up process keeps you at the top of it without damaging the relationship.

**Day 1 (due date):** No action. Give it the day.

**Day 2:** Send a polite email referencing the invoice number and due date. Keep it short.

> *"Hi [Name], just following up on Invoice #0042 for ₦200,000 which was due yesterday. Please let me know if you need anything from my end to process this."*

**Day 7:** Call, don't email. A two-minute phone call moves faster than three email threads.

**Day 14:** Send a formal reminder on letterhead stating the outstanding amount and that you reserve the right to pause future work until payment is received.

**Day 30+:** At this point, consider whether future work with this client requires a deposit. Some clients are chronically late — your payment terms should reflect that.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Sending one invoice at month-end for work done weeks ago.** Invoice promptly — the moment a project is delivered or a milestone is hit. Delay on your end signals the money isn't urgent.

**Vague line items.** "Consulting services — March" tells a client nothing. "Brand strategy session (4 hours × ₦25,000/hr)" is specific, verifiable, and harder to dispute.

**No invoice number.** Without a reference number, both you and your client can't track the document easily. Start at #001 and go up from there.

**Sending PDF invoices that require printing.** In 2026, your clients want to approve and forward digitally. Use tools that generate clean, shareable PDF invoices automatically.
